About TrustRank

Society is built on trust. Even the smartest and most capable people have to trust others, as we simply don’t have the time and resources to double-check everything others say. But of course not everyone is trustworthy, or trustworthy to the same degree. TrustRank’s goal is to make a formal accounting of who we trust and why.

Describing who we trust is not the same thing as proscribing who to trust -- that is, determining who or what is trustworthy. Although we expect that many people will use TrustRank to determine trustworthiness, we want to be as clear about this distinction as possible, so that our users can grasp the limitations of the tool. TrustRank is only as accurate as its users, and its users are, like all human beings, capable of making mistakes.

With that caveat out of the way, let’s dive into how the tool works.

The Trust Score

Users of TrustRank can submit statements to the site. A statement is a claim or set of claims hosted anywhere else online, including news stories, social media posts, government webpages, and more. These claims do not need to be a user’s own claims; in fact, a user has no special ownership or editing power towards their own claims, beyond a thirty minute window after creation. (Statements can be updated, but this process requires updating the statement source and submitting an update request to TrustRank.)

Every statement has a trust score. The score is calculated via a transparent algorithm (see “Core Algorithms” below) using trust ratings by TrustRank users of the specific statement, the claimant who made the statement, and the user who made the statement, if the claimant is associated with a TrustRank user.

At this point, it’s useful to distinguish between users and claimants. A user is any person who makes an account on the TrustRank site. A claimant is someone to whom one or more statements are attributed. You may have users who are also claimants, but you may also have users with no attributed statements and claimants with many attributed statements who are not themselves users of the site. As stated above, users have no special ownership of their own claims. The only link between a user and claimant is in how their trustworthiness scores influence each other.

A statement’s trust score is calculated via the following sources:

  • Whether or not the specific statement is trusted by TrustRank users
  • Whether or not the claimant (person or organization making the statement) is trusted by TrustRank users
  • If the claimant is a TrustRank user, whether that user is trusted by TrustRank users

Trust But Verify

At the heart of trust is verification. No one person can verify everything, but together we can assess the truth of all the important statements on which we base our decisions.

Users can create links between statements. This can be used to determine attribution, indicate that one statement contains another statement, and more. A key relationship between statements is verification. That is, users may add a statement and indicate that it verifies another.

For example, a user may see a statement describing a President's executive order. They may then add a statement linking to executive order's listing in the Federal Register, specifying that this new statement verifies the first.

Like all statements, statements which verify other statements may be rated for accuracy. They may also be verified themselves. "Ah!" you may be thinking. "So I can submit a verification of a verification of a verification? And then a verification of that? Is it verifications all the way down?”

In some sense, it is. But practically, we need to draw a line somewhere. Certain kinds of verifications are assumed to be true because they come from such trustworthy sources. We call these “base verifications”. These can be wrong and they can be challenged, but we call them “base verifications” because we believe they will almost always be correct. To provide some consequences to getting it wrong, the TrustRank organization will pay $300 to anyone who can show that a base verification is incorrect.

A given statement will have one of the following verification statuses:

  • Unverified: A statement for which no verifications have been submitted.
  • In process: A statement for which some verifications have been submitted, but for which no verification has been accepted (see below for definition of 'accepted').
  • Verified as True/Verified as False: A statement for which one or more verifications have been submitted and accepted. If more than one verification has been accepted, none are in conflict.
  • In conflict: A statement for which multiple verifications that been submitted and accepted, are in conflict with each other.
  • Base-verified: A statement which has been verified via base verification. The typical way to resolve a verification conflict is via a verification chain ending in a base verification.

For a verification to be “accepted”, it must be rated as trustworthy by at least two raters, with 66% or more of raters agreeing.

A statement’s trust score and verification status are combined into an overall reliability rank, to allow users to quickly and easily decide whether or not to trust a statement.

Facts vs Opinions

It is, of course, much easier to verify certain kinds of statements than others. Some people use the language of “fact” and “opinion”. They use “fact” to mean something provable or disprovable, and “opinion” to mean something that cannot be proved or disproved.

We consider opinions to be under-specified facts. Take, for example, the statement “Cats are better than dogs.” As is, it’s not provable. But, if you press the speaker, they might specify, “Cats are easier to take care of than dogs.” This is now a verifiable statement. You could, theoretically, do a survey of cat owners and dog owners asking the amount of time and effort they put into pet ownership. Or you could ask the speaker to specify further. Perhaps the heart of their claim is that “Cats cause less property damage than dogs”, which you can verify via patterns of insurance claims. (Or which you could decide you don’t care enough to verify!)

The site is built to encourage facts over opinions, by pushing users and claimants to specify their opinions as much as possible. Users can rate a statement as being low in specificity, and can provide questions for a claimant to address. A claimant, seeing that a statement of theirs has provoked controversy on TrustRank, can update or clarify a previous statement through the claim’s source.

In the meantime, the statement will be considered less trustworthy than a statement with a similar trust score but higher specificity, as a more specific statement is considered easier to verify. Since claimant’s trustworthiness is calculated in part from the trustworthiness of their statements, claimants with a pattern of making non-specific statements will have a lower trust score overall.

Protecting Against Abuse

A system of trust relies on the majority of users having good intentions towards the system. It does not require the majority of users to agree on politics or even on basic journalistic standards. Therefore, our signup and moderation policy is based on keeping out spammers and trolls, and preventing harassment of users, while keeping a hands-off approach to the actual content.

TrustRank has two tiers of users, Starred and Unstarred. Starred users are treated preferentially in two majors ways. First, their rankings are given greater weight when calculating trust scores. Secondly, they’re able to add an unlimited number of statements to the site, make an unlimited number of ratings, and leave an unlimited number of comments. The following individuals and organizations can automatically become Starred Users:

  • Credentialed news organizations of any kind
  • Elected or appointed government officials at any level of local, state, national or international government
  • Credentialed educational institutions, as well as individual administrators, faculty, or adjuncts at said institutions

Additional categories of users will be added to the list above.

The second tier of users, Unstarred Users, are accounts that can be created by anyone. They are limited in the number of statements, comments or ratings they can add per day, and their ratings count for less than those of Starred Users.

An Unstarred User may be promoted to a Starred User if their participation on the site demonstrates good faith over time. An Unstarred User may also be promoted to a Starred User if their professional circumstances change, for instance if they are elected to office or gain a byline at a credentialed news organization. Less commonly, a Starred User may be demoted to an Unstarred User if they lose their credentials or if they exhibit consistent bad behavior on the site.

Core Algorithms

Transparency is essential to verification. As such, we are committed to keeping our data, our algorithms, and our software public, and to providing easy-to-understand explanations of how everything works. Our algorithms page displays the algorithms the site is actively using to generate scores as well as past versions we've used.

Whenever an algorithm is updated, we will publicize the update. Periodically, we will 'immortalize' a version of an algorithm by calculating scores with that algorithm and providing those for an explicitly stated period of months or years. This is useful for third party sites who rely on scores being calculated in a specific way.

The following is a list of core algorithms currently provided by the site:

  • User Trust Score: provides a universal trust score for a given user
  • Statement Trust Score: provides a universal trust score for a given statement
  • StatementLink Trust Score: provides a universal trust score for a given link between two statements
  • Under-verified Statements Score: provides statements that especially need to be verified

The algorithms page has more detail on how they're currently implemented.

Miscellaneous Questions

Attributing Statements

Because claimants’ trust scores are influenced by their statements, and vice versa, it’s important that claimants be correctly associated with their statements. There are a few different obstacles here.

Incorrect user attributions: Because users can create statement-claimant links for anyone, there will inescapably be some incorrect attributions. Users may always flag an attribution as potentially incorrect. Attributions are not accepted by default. To be accepted, they must be either:

  • marked as correct by a Starred User
  • marked as correct by 66% of all users, with 2 or more users marking
  • marked as correct by a TrustRank admin

Attributing statements across multiple platforms: A claimant will be considered as a single entity across multiple accounts when:

  • There are two-way links between two different accounts (for instance, a Twitter profile linking to a newspaper byline, which links back to the Twitter profile)
  • The platforms are linked via a verification service like Keybase
  • A user-claimant verifies ownership of multiple platforms via the TrustRank organization

Attributing deleted statements: When statements are added to TrustRank, we make backups of the statements. For storage and performance purposes, we do not provide this information for users by default. Users who wish to challenge the attribution of a deleted statement can submit a request.

If a statement is incorrectly attributed to a claimant and then removed, the statement’s trust score or verification status will no longer be considered when calculating the user’s trust score.

Making Corrections

Everyone makes mistakes. Issuing corrections and retractions is one of the most important signs of trustworthiness, and at TrustRank we try to take this into account.

After a statement is verified as false, the claimant has 48 hours to update/correct their statement. (For claimants that are TrustRank users, we will send an email to the claimant letting them know that their statement has been verified as false and informing them of their 48 hour window.) If the statement is updated to match the verification status with that window, it will not be counted against their trustworthiness.

If the claimant believes that the verification is incorrect, they do not need to update their statement. They can dispute the verification by providing a counter-verification, or wait for others to do so. While their trust score may take a temporary dip, there is no long term impact to the user if one of their statements is verified as false and then refuted.

Contributing to TrustRank

Integrating TrustRank With Other Sites

Our goal is to create a service/utility to help individuals and organizations understand who and how they trust others. To that end, all of our data, code and algorithms are open. We provide our data via a rate-limited API.

We are enthusiastic about working with others to integrate TrustRank into their own tools and platforms.

Contributing to TrustRank

As this project is in its earliest phases, we do not have a formal contribution system set up. Informally, though, contributors are welcome! You can reach out to the project's founder at shaunagm@gmail.com. You can also check out the project's github account.
